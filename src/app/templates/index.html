About

GeoModelBuilder (GMB) is a tool for writing and solving systems of geometric constraints for olympiad-style geometry problems. The input to GMB is a problem statement (i.e., system of geometric constraints) written in the Geometry Model-Building Language (GMBL), a custom domain-specific language (DSL). The GMBL permits reasoning about three types of geometric objects: points, lines, and circles. GMB outputs diagrams (i.e., models for the constraint system) that satisfy the GMBL problem statement. The user can specify how many such models they would like to generate, and can subsequently download information about these models for inspection. GMB is also made available as a command-line interface for integration with other tools.

This work was motivated by an ongoing effort to complete the IMO Grand Challenge -- an effort to build an AI that can win a gold medal at the International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO). The models generated by GMB are intended to guide solvers for such geometry problems, though it may have uses in other domains (e.g., education, computer-aided design). For technical details, please refer to the arXiv paper.

Tutorial

The following tutorial describes the GMBL, a DSL in which users write geometry problem statements for model generation. This DSL can broadly be summarized in two parts:

  1. A set of commands to construct a numerical optimization problem to solve for parameterized points, lines, and circles
  2. A set of functions and predicates to define points/lines/circles/numerical values and relationships between them to serve as arguments to the aforementioned commands

All commands, predicates, and function symbols follow Lisp-style s-expression format.

Preliminaries

This tutorial makes use of the following notation:
  • The type of a geometric object can take one of three values: point, line, or circle
  • We use <> to denote an instance of a type. For example, point is an instance of type while <point> is an instance of point
  • A name is a string value that refers to a geometric object
    • names are assigned to geometric objects with the define and param commands (see Commands)
    Example: If the name P is assigned to the midpoint of two points A and B (e.g., with (define P point (midp A B))), P is an instance of point
  • pred and func are used as shorthand for predicate and function, respectively
    • .
Throughout the tutorial, we also refer to the following geometric objects:
  • A, B, C, and D are instances of point
  • L1, L2, and L3 are instances of line
  • C1 and C2 are instances of circle
Names of geometric objects are case sensitive. All other identifiers in the GMBL (e.g., commands, types, functions, predicates) are case insensitive:
  • Example: (define P (midp A B)) and (DEFINE P (MiDp A B)) are equivalent
  • Example: (define P (midp A B)) and (define p (midp A B)) are not equivalent

Commands

GMB generates models by translating a GMBL program into a numerical optimization problem and solving it with off-the-shelf optimizers. The following four commands reflect this, and comprise the primary component of the GMBL

  • param: Assigns a name to a geometric object parameterized either by the default parameterization for <type> or by a supplied parameterization

    (param <string> <type> <optional-parameterization>)

    Each type has the following default parameterization:
    • point: Parameterized by its x- and y-coordinates
    • line: Parameterized by two points that define the line
    • circle: Parameterized by its origin and radius (where the origin is parameterized as a point)

    The following optional parameterizations can be supplied to...
    • ... parameterize a point:
      • (on-line L1): Parameterizes a point on line L1
      • (on-seg A B): Parameterizes a point on the segment AB
      • (on-ray A B): Parameterizes a point on the ray starting at A and extending through B
      • (on-ray-opp A B): Parameterizes a point beyond A on the ray starting at B extending beyond A
      • (on-circ C1): Parameterizes a point on the circle C1
      • (in-poly P1 ... PN): Parameterizes a point inside the polygon with vertices P1 ... PN, where N >= 3 and P1 ... PN are declared points
    • ... parameterize a LINE:
      • (tangent-lc C1): Parameterizes a line that is tangent to C1
      • (through A): Parameterizes a line that passes through A
    • ... parameterize a CIRCLE:
      • (tangent-cc C1): Parameterizes a circle that is tangent to C1
      • (tangent-cl L1): Parameterizes a circle that is tangent to L1
      • (through A): Parameterizes a circle passing through A
      • (origin A): Parameterized a circle centered at A
      • (radius N1): Parameterized a circle with radius N1, where N1 is an instance of number


    Valid Usage
    • (param P point)
      • Introduces a point P parameterized by its coordinates
    • (param Gamma circle (origin A))
      • Introduces a circle Gamma that is centered at A and is parameterized by it's radius
    • (param P point (on-circ C1))
      • Introduces a point P parameterized on C1
    • (param Gamma circle)
      • Introduces a circle Gamma parameterized by its origin and radius
    • (param L line (through A))
      • Introduces a line L that passes through the point A
    Invalid Usage
    • (param X number)
      • Numerical values cannot be named
    • (param P point (incircle A B C))
      • (incircle A B C) is not a valid parameterization of a point
    • (param P point (on-circ C1))
      (param P point)
      • Cannot introduce the same object twice


    In addition to the standard grammar for parameterizations outlined above, there is an important variation of param to introduce sets of points that form triangles and polygons. These variations take the following form:

    (param (<string>, <string>, ..., <string>) <parameterization>)

    Note the omission of type information -- the type of (point, point, ..., point) is implied. While it could be standard to infer type in a param (or define) command when possible, it is generally enforced to improve readability.

    The following values of <parameterization> are available:
    • triangle: Parameterize a list of three points as a triangle
    • acute-tri: Parameterize a list of three points as an acute triangle
    • (iso-tri <string>): Parameterize a list of three points as an isosceles triangle. <string> must be in the list of point names being parameterized. For example, (param (P Q R) (iso-tri P)) introduces an isosceles triangle with PQ = PR
    • (acute-iso-tri <string>): Parameterize a list of three points as an acute isosceles triangle per the above description of an isosceles parameterization
    • (right-tri <string>): Parameterize a list of three points as a right triangle. <string> must be in the list of point names being parameterized. For example, (param (P Q R) (right-tri P)) introduces a triangle with a right angle at <QPR
    • polygon: Parameterize a list of n points as a convex polygon


    Valid Usage
    • (param (P Q R) triangle)
      • Introduces three points, P, Q, and R that form a triangle
    • (PAraM (P Q R) trIaNglE)
      • All identifiers other than names of geometric objects are case insensitive
    • (param (P Q R) (iso-tri Q))
      • Introduce three points that form an isosceles triangle with QP = QR
    • (param (P Q R S T) polygon)
      • Introduces five points, P, Q, R, S, and T, that form a convex pentagon
    Invalid Usage
    • (param (P Q R S) triangle)
      • Only lists of points of length three can be parameterized as a triangle
    • (param (P Q R) (iso-tri A))
      • The argument to iso-tri must be a point in the isosceles triangle (i.e., a point being introduced)
    • (param (P Q R) (iso-tri p))
      • Names of geometric objects are case sensitive
    • (param (P Q) polygon)
      • A polygon has at least three vertices
    • (param (P Q R) (point point point) triangle)
      • Type information should not be included for commands introducing lists of points that form triangles/polygons
    • (param (P Q R))
      • When parameterizing a list of points, <parameterization> is required
  • define: Assigns a name to a geometric object computed from existing objects. <value> is one of <point>, <line>, or <circle>.

    (define <string> <type> <value>)


    Valid Usage
    • (define P point (incenter A B C))
      • P is assigned to the incenter of a triangle with vertices A, B, and C
    • (define Gamma circle (incircle A B C))
      • Gamma is assigned to the incircle of a triangle with vertices A, B, and C
    • (define L line (perp-bis B C))
      • L is assigned to the perpendicular bisector of the segment connecting B and C
    Invalid Usage
    • (define X number (dist A B))
      • Numerical values cannot be named
    • (define P point (incircle A B C))
      • The type of <value> must be consistent with <type>
    • (define P point A)
      • <value> cannot be a name as duplicate objects are not permitted

    Note that detail on functions used in the above examples (e.g., incenter, dist) is provided in the next section

  • assert: Imposes an additional constraint <pred> on the system. This command does not introduce any new geometric objects and can only refer to those that have already been introduced by either define or param.

    (assert <pred>)


    Valid Usage
    • (assert (para L1 L2))
      • Assert that line L1 is parallel to line L2
    • (assert (not (coll A B C)))
      • Assert that points A, B, and C are not collinear
    • (assert (= 4.0 (dist A B)))
      • Numerical values find their place in assert. The above asserts that the distance between points A and B is 4.0
    Invalid Usage
    • (assert (para L1 L2) (not (coll A B C)))
      • Assertions are made one at a time
    • (assert (coll P Q R))
      • Assertions cannot introduce geometric objects, and P, Q, and R have not been assigned (see Preliminaries)

    Detail on supported predicates (e.g., para, coll) is provided in the following section

    .
  • eval: Evaluates a given constraint in the final model(s). Like assert, this command does not introduce any new geometric objects.

    (eval <pred>)

    Usage for eval is the same as that for assert. The key distinction between assert and eval is that eval does not enforce <pred> via numerical optimization. This command is most useful for those interested in integrating GMB with automated theorem provers. As many problem statements are of the form "If X, prove Y," one may wish to optimize directly for X while only checking that Y holds in the final model.

Functions and Predicates

Per the above section, GMBL commands accept points, lines, circles, numerical values, and predicates as arguments. In the following, we provide a sample of the functions and predicates by which a user can define such arguments. For a complete list, please reference the appendices in our arXiv paper.

Functions

Functions construct new geometric objects, as well as numerical values for dimensional constraints, from ones already declared. Functions in the GMBL include a unique feature -- root selectors. Root selectors are included to address the issue that when geometric constraints are translated to numerical ones, many solutions often exist which are degenerate in their geometric interpretation. In plane geometry, this problem typically manifests with multiple candidate POINT solutions, such as the intersection between a line and a circle.

To control for this, we introduce a set of root selectors for plane geometry to be used in several functions of type point:

  • rs-arbitrary: An arbitrary root
  • (rs-neq A): The root that does not equal A
  • (rs-opp-sides A L1): The root on the opposite side of L1 as A
  • (rs-same-side A L1): The root on the same side of L1 as A
The functions inter-lc and inter-cc require a root selector.

Keeping this concept in mind, we now introduce a set of commonly used functions to...

  • ... construct a point
    • (midp A B): The midpoint of A and B
    • (inter-ll L1 L2): The intersection of L1 and L2
    • (circumcenter A B C): The circumcenter of the triangle with vertices A, B, and C
    • (orthocenter A B C): The orthocenter of the triangle with vertices A, B, and C
    • (inter-lc L1 C1 <root-selector>): The intersection of L1 and C1 as specified by a root selector
  • ... construct a line
    • (line A B): The line connecting A and B
    • (perp-bis A B): The perpendicular bisector of line AB
    • (perp-at A L1): The line through A that is perpendicular to L1
  • ... construct a circle
    • (circ A B C): The circle passing through A, B, and C
    • (incircle A B C): The incircle of the triangle with vertices A, B, and C
    • (diam A B): The circle with diameter AB
  • ... construct a number
    • (dist A B): The length of the segment AB
    • (uangle A B C): The undirected angle measure ∠ABC
    • (add/sub/mul/div/pow N1 N2): The standard binary operations, where N1 and N2 are instances of number
    • (neg/sqrt N1): The standard unary operations, where N1 is an instance of number

Predicates

Predicates describe relationships between geometric objects or numerical values. As with functions, here we provide a sample of commonly used predicates and the reader should refer to our arXiv paper for a full list:

  • (cong A B C D): True i.f.f. the segments AB and CD are congruent
  • (coll A B C): True i.f.f. A, B, and C are collinear
  • (on-line A L1): True i.f.f. A is on line L1
  • (on-circ A C1): True i.f.f. A is on circle C1
  • (on-seg C A B): True i.f.f. C is on the segment AB
  • (on-ray C A B): True i.f.f. C is on the ray starting at A that extends through B
  • (concur L1 L2 L3): True i.f.f. lines L1, L2, and L3 intersect at a common point

Example Usage

Here we provide examples of valid and invalid usage of functions and predicates to construct arguments for commands:

Valid Usage
  • (param P point (on-seg A (midp A B)))
    • Parameterize a point P on the segment connecting A and (midp A B)
  • (define P point (inter-lc L1 C1 rs-arbitrary))
    • Compute a point P as any intersection of the line L1 with the circle C1
  • (define Q point (inter-lc L1 C1 (rs-neq P)))
    • Compute a point Q as the intersection of the line L1 with the circle C1 that is not P
  • (param Gamma circle (origin (inter-ll L1 (perp-bis A B))))
    • Parameterize a circle Gamma whose origin is the intersection of L1 and the perpendicular bisector of the segment AB
  • (assert (= C (midp A B)))
    • Assert that C is the midpoint of the segment AB
Invalid Usage
  • (assert (inter-ll L1 L2))
    • Assertions take predicates as arguments, not functions
  • (define P point (inter-lc L1 C1))
    • Root selectors are required, even if you do not care which root is selected
  • (assert (= A B))
    • While technically a valid command, duplicate points are not allowed so this assertion will prevent a model from being found

The following examples are adapted from previous IMO problems, as well as problems found in Evan Chen's "Euclidean Geometry in Mathematical Olympiads" (EGMO).

IMO 2019, Problem 2

In a triangle ABC, point A1 lies on side BC and point B1 lies on side AC. Let P and Q be points on segments AA1 and BB1, respectively, such that PQ is parallel to AB. Let P1 be a point on line PB1, such that B1 lies strictly between P and P1, and ∠PP1C = ∠BAC. Similarly, let A1 be a point on line QA1, such that A1 lies strictly between Q and Q1, and ∠CQ1Q = ∠CBA. Prove that points P, Q, P1, and Q1 are concyclic.

Let ABC be an acute triangle with circumcircle Γ. Let ℓ be a tangent line to Γ, and let ℓa, ℓb, and ℓc be the lines obtained by reflecting ℓ in the lines BC, CA, and AB, respectively. Show that the circumcircle of the triangle determined by the lines ℓa, ℓb, and ℓc is tangent to the circle Γ.

IMO 2008, Problem 1

An acute-angled triangle ABC has orthocenter H. The circle passing through H with centre the midpoint of BC intersects the line BC at A1 and A2. Similarly, the circle passing through H with centre the midpoint of CA intersects the line CA at B1 and B2, and the circle passing through H with centre the midpoint of AB intersects the line AB at C1 and C2.
